# Fair Wear and Tear in Tenancy Deposit Disputes

Fair wear and tear refers to the natural deterioration of a property and its contents through normal everyday use during a tenancy. In tenancy deposit disputes, adjudicators distinguish between expected ageing and damage caused by tenant negligence, misuse, or failure to maintain the property properly.

Understanding fair wear and tear is important because landlords cannot legally charge tenants for normal deterioration that occurs over time. Deposit deductions must instead relate to damage, excessive dirtiness, or avoidable deterioration beyond what would reasonably be expected.

## Why Fair Wear and Tear Matters

When a tenancy ends, disputes often arise regarding cleaning, repairs, decoration, or replacement costs. Evidence is central to determining whether deterioration is considered normal wear or tenant-caused damage.

The deposit legally belongs to the tenant unless the landlord can provide evidence supporting deductions. Adjudicators rely entirely on the documentation provided and will not visit the property in person.

To assess claims fairly, adjudicators compare the property's condition at the start and end of the tenancy using inventories, check-in reports, checkout reports, photographs, invoices, and communication records.

## How Adjudicators Assess Wear and Tear

Adjudicators consider several factors when deciding whether deterioration is reasonable:

- length of tenancy
- number of occupants
- age and quality of fixtures or furnishings
- expected lifespan of items
- level of use during normal occupation

For example, slight carpet flattening after several years is normally considered fair wear and tear. However, large stains, burns, or tears caused by negligence may justify a deduction.

Similarly, minor scuff marks on walls are generally expected over time, while large holes, graffiti, or excessive damage may be considered tenant liability.

## Examples of Fair Wear and Tear

Examples commonly treated as fair wear and tear include:

- faded paint due to sunlight
- minor carpet wear in high-traffic areas
- loose door handles from normal use
- small marks on walls
- gradual appliance ageing
- minor limescale accumulation consistent with normal use

These forms of deterioration are expected during occupation and do not normally justify compensation claims.

## Examples of Tenant-Caused Damage

Examples more likely to justify deductions include:

- cigarette burns on carpets
- broken appliances caused by misuse
- large stains or pet damage
- excessive grease and dirt requiring specialist cleaning
- missing furniture or fixtures
- broken doors, windows, or fittings

The landlord must still prove the extent of the deterioration and demonstrate that the costs claimed are reasonable.

## Importance of Comparative Evidence

Comparative evidence is critical in tenancy disputes. A landlord must usually provide:

- signed inventory reports
- check-in and checkout reports
- date-stamped photographs
- invoices or contractor estimates
- tenancy agreement clauses

Without a signed inventory or reliable check-in evidence, it becomes significantly harder to prove tenant responsibility.

For example, checkout photographs alone may show dirt or damage, but they do not prove the condition was better at the start of the tenancy.

## Cleaning and Condition Disputes

Cleaning disputes are one of the most common tenancy deposit disagreements. Adjudicators assess whether the property was returned in a reasonably similar condition to the start of the tenancy, accounting for normal living conditions and tenancy duration.

Landlords cannot automatically charge for professional cleaning unless they can demonstrate deterioration beyond fair wear and tear. Evidence showing the property was professionally cleaned before move-in may strengthen a landlord’s position.

## Common Evidence Problems

Claims are frequently weakened by:

- missing inventories
- poor quality photographs
- inconsistent invoices
- lack of written communication
- delayed checkout inspections

Incomplete evidence reduces the likelihood of successful deductions.

## Key Principle

Fair wear and tear recognises that rented properties naturally deteriorate through ordinary use. Deposit deductions should only compensate landlords for avoidable damage or excessive deterioration caused during the tenancy.

Clear, organised, and comparative evidence remains the most important factor in resolving disputes fairly.
