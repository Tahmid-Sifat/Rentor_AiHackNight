# Tenant Fees and Prohibited Charges Under the Tenant Fees Act

The Tenant Fees Act introduced restrictions on the charges landlords and letting agents can impose on tenants in England. The legislation aims to prevent unfair costs during the tenancy process and improve transparency within the private rented sector.

Under the Act, most fees connected with granting, renewing, or continuing a tenancy are prohibited unless specifically permitted by law. Tenants who are charged unlawful fees may have the right to recover the money and challenge the charge through enforcement authorities or legal processes.

## Purpose of the Tenant Fees Act

The legislation was introduced to reduce excessive upfront costs faced by tenants and to prevent landlords or agents from charging unnecessary administration or service fees.

The rules apply primarily to assured shorthold tenancies, student accommodation, and licences to occupy housing in England.

Landlords and letting agents must ensure that any payments requested from tenants fall within the categories allowed by the legislation.

## Permitted Payments

Only certain payments are legally permitted under the Tenant Fees Act. These commonly include:

- rent
- tenancy deposits (subject to deposit caps)
- holding deposits
- payments for utilities, communication services, and council tax where specified in the tenancy agreement
- charges for late rent payments in limited circumstances
- replacement costs for lost keys or security devices where reasonable
- fees linked to tenant-requested contract changes where permitted by law

Any permitted payment must generally be clearly stated in the tenancy agreement.

## Prohibited Fees

Charges that are not specifically authorised by the legislation are prohibited.

Examples of unlawful or prohibited fees may include:

- administration fees
- referencing fees
- credit check fees
- inventory fees
- contract renewal fees
- check-in or checkout charges
- viewing fees
- cleaning fees imposed automatically without evidence
- excessive charges for replacing keys or documents

Landlords and agents cannot require tenants to make payments simply as a condition of entering, renewing, or ending a tenancy unless the charge is legally permitted.

## Administration Fee Restrictions

Administration fees were one of the main targets of the legislation. Before the Act, tenants were often charged significant amounts for activities such as:

- preparing tenancy agreements
- referencing
- background checks
- renewing contracts
- processing paperwork

Under the Tenant Fees Act, these routine administrative costs are generally no longer recoverable from tenants.

Any attempt to disguise prohibited administration fees under another description may still be considered unlawful.

## Tenant Rights and Enforcement

Tenants who believe they have been charged prohibited fees may have several options available.

Potential remedies can include:

- requesting repayment directly from the landlord or agent
- reporting the matter to local authority trading standards teams
- seeking assistance from tenancy advice organisations
- recovering prohibited payments through legal processes

A landlord or letting agent who breaches the legislation may face financial penalties and restrictions on serving certain eviction notices until unlawful fees are repaid.

## Holding Deposits

Holding deposits are permitted but regulated. Landlords and agents must usually return the holding deposit within specific timeframes unless lawful reasons exist for retaining it.

Disputes may arise where a landlord claims the tenant provided misleading information or withdrew from the tenancy process.

## Deposits and Additional Charges

Tenancy deposits remain permitted but are subject to legal caps. Landlords must also comply with tenancy deposit protection rules, including protecting deposits within an authorised scheme.

Separate charges for damage, cleaning, or unpaid rent may still arise at the end of the tenancy, but landlords must normally justify these deductions with evidence.

## Practical Compliance Considerations

For dispute resolution purposes, useful evidence may include:

- tenancy agreements
- invoices or receipts
- written communication
- payment records
- screenshots of advertised fees
- emails discussing charges

A clear written record helps establish whether a fee was disclosed properly and whether it was legally permitted.

## Key Principle

Under the Tenant Fees Act, tenants should only be asked to pay charges that are specifically allowed by law. Most routine administration and processing fees are prohibited, and landlords or letting agents must be able to justify any additional payments requested during the tenancy process.
