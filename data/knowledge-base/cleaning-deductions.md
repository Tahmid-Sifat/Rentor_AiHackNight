# Cleaning Deductions

## The Core Principle

A landlord can only deduct for cleaning if the property was left in a worse condition than it was received, accounting for fair wear and tear. The landlord cannot charge for cleaning simply because they want professional cleaning done — they must show actual additional cleaning was required.

## Check-In Condition Is Critical

The check-in inventory is the baseline for any cleaning dispute. If the property was not professionally cleaned at the start of the tenancy:

- The landlord cannot demand professional cleaning at the end
- The tenant only needs to return the property to the same standard as received
- Any check-in inventory noting dirty oven, limescale, or grease is strong evidence for the tenant

## What Landlords Must Prove

To justify a cleaning deduction, the landlord should provide:

1. Evidence that the property was professionally cleaned at check-in (invoice or statement)
2. Evidence of the specific cleaning required at check-out (check-out report with photos)
3. An actual invoice or receipt for the cleaning carried out
4. Evidence that the cleaning cost was reasonable and competitive

## Common Cleaning Disputes

### Oven Cleaning
- If the oven had grease marks at check-in, the tenant only needs to return it to that standard
- A £100+ oven cleaning charge is often disproportionate for light use marks
- Professional oven cleaning is typically £50-80

### Carpet Cleaning
- If carpets were described as "worn" or "stained" at check-in, charges are harder to justify
- End-of-tenancy carpet cleaning is not always required
- Normal dust and light marks are wear and tear

### General Deep Clean
- Charges of £300-500 for a standard flat are often excessive
- Tenants should request itemised invoices
- Compare the check-in inventory condition with the check-out report

## Key Evidence to Request

Tenants should always ask the landlord or agent for:

- Check-in cleaning evidence (invoice or inventory statement)
- Check-out report with photos
- Cleaning invoice with company name, date, and itemised costs
- Any comparison photos taken at check-in vs. check-out

## Proportionality

Even if cleaning is needed, the charge must be proportionate to actual loss. A landlord cannot charge for a full deep clean of the entire property if only one room required additional cleaning.

## Template Response Points

- "Please provide evidence that the property was professionally cleaned at the start of the tenancy."
- "Please provide a copy of the cleaning invoice showing itemised costs."
- "The check-in inventory notes [condition] which suggests this level of cleaning was pre-existing."
- "I dispute the cleaning charge as the property was returned in the same standard as received."
