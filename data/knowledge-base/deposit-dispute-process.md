# Deposit Dispute Process

## Step-by-Step Dispute Process

### Step 1: Respond to Deduction Proposal

When a landlord proposes deductions:

- Do NOT agree to release any disputed amount
- Respond in writing (email is fine) within a reasonable time (typically 10 working days)
- State clearly which deductions you dispute and why
- Ask for supporting evidence for each deduction

### Step 2: Request Evidence

For each deduction, request:

- Invoices or receipts for work done
- Dated photographs (before and after where relevant)
- Check-in and check-out inventory/reports
- Evidence of what condition the property was in at the start of the tenancy

### Step 3: Negotiate

Try to reach a reasonable settlement. If the landlord has some valid evidence:

- Consider whether a partial payment is appropriate
- Do not agree to an amount higher than reasonably justified
- Always get any agreement in writing

### Step 4: Involve the Deposit Scheme

If you cannot reach agreement within 10 days of the end of the tenancy:

- Contact your deposit protection scheme
- Request the disputed amount be protected pending resolution
- Apply to the scheme's Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) service

Both parties must consent to ADR, but it is free and binding.

### Step 5: ADR Submission

When submitting to ADR:

- Submit all your evidence (check-in inventory, photos, emails, invoices you have received)
- Write a clear statement explaining each dispute
- Be factual and professional
- Reference the check-in condition for each item disputed

### Step 6: Adjudicator Decision

An independent adjudicator will:

- Review all evidence from both sides
- Make a decision based on the evidence
- Award amounts based on actual loss and betterment principles

The decision is binding on both parties.

## Key Timelines

| Event | Deadline |
|---|---|
| Deposit must be protected | Within 30 days of receipt |
| Prescribed Information must be provided | Within 30 days of receipt |
| Deposit must be returned (agreed amount) | Within 10 days of agreement |
| Landlord must return deposit at end if no dispute raised | Typically 10 days |

## What to Include in Your Dispute Letter

A good dispute letter should:

1. Reference the property address and tenancy dates
2. State the total deposit amount
3. List each deduction you dispute with reasons
4. Request specific evidence for each charge
5. State that you do not consent to release of disputed amount
6. Reference relevant laws if applicable (Tenant Fees Act, fair wear and tear)
7. Set a reasonable deadline for response (10-14 days)
8. Be polite and professional in tone

## Evidence Hierarchy (Strongest to Weakest)

1. Signed check-in inventory showing pre-existing condition
2. Dated photographs taken at check-in
3. Landlord's own check-out report showing damage
4. Professional invoices or receipts
5. Emails and written communications
6. Witness statements
7. Unsigned or undated documents

## Small Claims Court

If the deposit was not protected, or the landlord refuses to engage with ADR, the tenant can apply to the County Court using the small claims process. Court fees apply, but are recovered if successful.
