# Fair Wear and Tear

## Definition

Fair wear and tear refers to the normal deterioration of a property and its contents that occurs through ordinary, reasonable use during the tenancy. Landlords cannot charge tenants for fair wear and tear.

The longer the tenancy and the more occupants, the greater the expected wear and tear.

## What Counts as Fair Wear and Tear

- Small scuffs, marks, and scratches on walls and paintwork
- Faded or worn carpets in high-traffic areas (hallways, living rooms, stairs)
- Minor carpet indentations from furniture
- Small nail holes from hanging pictures
- Faded curtains or blinds
- Worn light switches and door handles
- Light limescale in bathrooms and kitchens
- Minor discolouration of grout
- Small chips on skirting boards and door frames
- Faded paint from sunlight
- Worn or faded upholstery from normal use

## What Does NOT Count as Fair Wear and Tear

- Burns, stains, or large tears in carpets
- Large holes in walls
- Broken fixtures and fittings
- Damage caused by neglect or misuse
- Pet damage (scratches, stains, odours)
- Deliberate damage
- Broken appliances due to misuse
- Significant mould caused by poor ventilation by tenant
- Graffiti or unauthorised painting

## Decoration / Repainting

Repainting is one of the most commonly disputed charges. Key principles:

- Normal scuffs and marks on walls are wear and tear, especially in a long tenancy
- A landlord cannot charge for full repainting after a 2-year tenancy simply because paint looks worn
- If walls were already scuffed or marked at check-in, no repainting charge should apply
- A landlord must show that damage beyond wear and tear occurred
- Cost of repainting must be proportionate — you cannot charge for a full repaint if only one wall needs attention

## Age and Depreciation

When an item needs replacing, its age must be considered. If a carpet was already 5 years old at the start of a 2-year tenancy, the tenant should only pay for the remaining useful life, not the full replacement cost.

The government Betterment Principle: a landlord cannot profit from a deposit claim by replacing an old item with a brand new one at the tenant's full expense.

## Adjudicator Approach

Deposit scheme adjudicators apply betterment principles when calculating awards. They typically:

- Reduce claims for old or worn items
- Reject charges where check-in condition already showed the issue
- Require landlords to provide evidence of actual damage beyond wear and tear

## Tenancy Length Guidance (Rough Guide)

| Item | Expected Lifespan | Wear and Tear Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Carpet (mid-range) | 10 years | 10% per year |
| Paintwork | 5-7 years | 15-20% per year |
| Curtains/blinds | 5 years | 20% per year |
| Upholstered furniture | 7-10 years | 10-15% per year |

These are guides, not fixed rules, but adjudicators use similar frameworks.
