# Landlord Deposit Claims and Proof Requirements

## Overview

Landlords and letting agents may seek deductions from a tenancy deposit where they have suffered financial loss caused by breaches of the tenancy agreement.

Common claim categories include:

* rent arrears
* cleaning costs
* repairs and redecoration
* replacement of damaged or missing items
* garden maintenance

Any deduction must be reasonable, proportionate, and supported by evidence.

Adjudicators assess whether the landlord has demonstrated entitlement to deductions using documentary and photographic evidence.

---

# Burden of Proof

In tenancy deposit disputes, the burden of proof rests with the landlord.

The tenancy deposit legally remains the tenant’s money unless the landlord can demonstrate:

1. a breach of the tenancy agreement
2. deterioration beyond fair wear and tear
3. financial loss suffered
4. that the proposed deduction is reasonable

Without sufficient evidence, disputed amounts may be returned to the tenant.

---

# Reasonable vs Unreasonable Claims

## Reasonable Claims

Reasonable claims compensate the landlord for actual financial loss caused by the tenant.

Adjudicators may award:

* repair costs
* cleaning costs
* partial replacement costs
* compensation for reduced lifespan or value

Where replacement is unnecessary, repair or cleaning may be considered more appropriate.

---

## Unreasonable Claims

Landlords must not seek to improve the property at the tenant’s expense.

This principle is known as betterment.

Examples of unreasonable claims may include:

* charging full replacement cost for an old carpet
* replacing lower-quality items with premium alternatives
* charging for professional cleaning where cleaning defects already existed at check-in

Claims must reflect the age, quality, and condition of the item at tenancy start.

---

# Fair Wear and Tear

Fair wear and tear refers to deterioration caused by ordinary use over time.

The House of Lords defines fair wear and tear as:

> “reasonable use of the premises by the tenant and the ordinary operation of natural forces”

Adjudicators commonly consider:

* length of tenancy
* number and age of occupants
* quality and lifespan of items
* ordinary household usage

Examples of likely fair wear and tear include:

* minor carpet wear
* faded furnishings
* light wall scuffs
* gradual appliance deterioration

---

# Wear and Tear vs Damage

Adjudicators distinguish between ordinary deterioration and tenant-caused damage.

## Examples More Likely to Be Fair Wear and Tear

* light carpet marks from normal use
* minor scuffing
* gradual fading
* ordinary ageing of fixtures

## Examples More Likely to Be Tenant Damage

* nail varnish spills
* iron burns
* negligent breakage
* severe staining
* deliberate or careless damage

Claims should reflect whether deterioration resulted from ordinary use or negligence.

---

# Evidence Expectations for Landlords

Strong evidence is essential for successful deposit claims.

Adjudicators rely entirely on submitted evidence and do not usually visit the property.

---

# Core Evidence Types

## Signed Tenancy Agreement

The tenancy agreement establishes:

* tenant obligations
* cleaning responsibilities
* maintenance terms
* contractual breaches

This document forms the foundation of most claims.

---

## Signed Check-In and Check-Out Reports

Comparative inventories are among the strongest forms of evidence.

These reports allow adjudicators to compare:

* property condition at move-in
* property condition at move-out

Signed reports generally carry greater evidential weight than unsigned documents.

Claims for cleaning or damage may fail where reliable comparative evidence is missing.

---

## Periodic Inspection Reports

Mid-tenancy inspection reports may support claims by documenting:

* developing deterioration
* maintenance issues
* ongoing property condition

These reports can strengthen timelines and corroborate other evidence.

---

## Communication Records

Emails, letters, and messages may help establish:

* repair reporting
* admissions of liability
* agreements between parties
* timelines of events

Written communication can support or weaken responsibility arguments.

---

## Invoices, Estimates, and Receipts

Financial evidence should support all monetary claims.

Useful documents include:

* contractor invoices
* repair receipts
* cleaning invoices
* replacement quotations
* gardening costs

Where possible, invoices should itemise labour and materials.

Adjudicators assess whether costs are proportionate to the damage demonstrated.

---

## Photographs and Videos

Date-stamped visual evidence is highly persuasive.

Photographs should:

* clearly show the issue
* identify affected areas
* include explanatory context where necessary
* compare condition before and after the tenancy

Poor-quality or unclear images may reduce evidential value.

---

## Witness Statements

Witness statements from contractors, surveyors, or third parties may provide supporting evidence.

Adjudicators may consider written statements but do not normally contact witnesses directly.

---

# Practical Cleaning and Repair Guidance

## Cleaning Claims

Tenants are generally expected to return the property cleaned to the same standard as at check-in.

Adjudicators consider:

* size and type of property
* extent of cleaning required
* original cleanliness standard

Standardised cleaning charges may be reduced if disproportionate to the actual condition.

Landlords may perform cleaning themselves and support the claim using contractor quotations showing comparable market costs.

---

## Repair and Redecoration Claims

Repair or redecoration claims should be proportionate and account for:

* fair wear and tear
* item lifespan
* age and quality
* existing deterioration

Full replacement may only be justified where damage is extensive or beyond economic repair.

---

# Key Principle

Successful tenancy deposit claims depend on clear, comparative, and proportionate evidence.

Adjudicators assess whether the landlord has demonstrated genuine financial loss beyond fair wear and tear using reliable inventories, photographs, invoices, tenancy agreements, and communication records.
