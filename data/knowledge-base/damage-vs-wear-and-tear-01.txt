# Tenant Damage vs Fair Wear and Tear

## Overview

In tenancy deposit disputes, adjudicators must distinguish between:

* fair wear and tear caused by ordinary use of the property
* tenant damage caused by negligence, misuse, accident, or breach of tenancy obligations

This distinction is central to deposit adjudication in England and Wales.

The tenancy deposit remains the tenant’s money unless the landlord proves, on the balance of probabilities, that the tenant caused loss beyond fair wear and tear. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Definition of Fair Wear and Tear

The House of Lords defines fair wear and tear as:

> “reasonable use of the premises by the tenant and the ordinary operation of natural forces” ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

Fair wear and tear refers to deterioration that occurs naturally through normal occupation over time.

Examples commonly treated as fair wear and tear include:

* worn carpets from foot traffic
* faded paint or curtains
* minor wall scuffs
* loose hinges or handles from ordinary use
* slight surface scratches
* furniture indentations in carpets

These issues are generally not chargeable to tenants. ([mydeposits][2])

---

# Definition of Tenant Damage

Tenant damage refers to deterioration caused by negligence, carelessness, misuse, accidental damage, or unauthorised actions.

Examples commonly treated as damage include:

* iron burns on carpets
* nail varnish spills
* deep scratches or gouges
* broken fixtures
* large wall holes
* burns, stains, or tears
* missing items
* unauthorised redecoration

Damage may justify deductions where supported by evidence. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

# Practical Tenancy Examples

| Issue                                               | Likely Classification |
| --------------------------------------------------- | --------------------- |
| Carpet flattened after 4-year tenancy               | Fair wear and tear    |
| Carpet stained with bleach                          | Tenant damage         |
| Minor wall scuffs in hallway                        | Fair wear and tear    |
| Large holes from mounted shelves without permission | Tenant damage         |
| Fading curtains from sunlight                       | Fair wear and tear    |
| Broken internal door from impact                    | Tenant damage         |
| Worn kitchen worktops from ordinary use             | Fair wear and tear    |
| Cigarette burns on flooring                         | Tenant damage         |

Adjudicators assess each case individually based on evidence and context. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Factors Considered by Adjudicators

There is no fixed formula for calculating fair wear and tear.

Adjudicators commonly consider:

## Length of Tenancy

Longer tenancies naturally result in greater deterioration.

## Number and Type of Occupants

Larger households and families with children generally create more ordinary wear than single occupants.

## Age and Quality of Items

Older or lower-quality items are expected to deteriorate faster through ordinary use.

## Expected Lifespan

Adjudicators assess whether an item had already partially depreciated before the tenancy ended. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Betterment

Landlords cannot use deposit deductions to improve the property or obtain “new for old” replacement value.

This principle is known as betterment.

Examples of unreasonable betterment include:

* charging full replacement cost for an old carpet
* upgrading damaged items to superior replacements at tenant expense
* claiming professional cleaning where the property was not professionally cleaned at check-in

Adjudicators may reduce awards to reflect depreciation, lifespan, and ordinary use. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Evidence Requirements in Deposit Disputes

Deposit disputes are evidence-based.

Strong evidence commonly includes:

* signed tenancy agreements
* signed check-in inventories
* signed check-out reports
* date-stamped photographs or videos
* inspection reports
* invoices and receipts
* correspondence between landlord and tenant
* witness statements

Comparative evidence showing property condition at both the start and end of the tenancy is especially important. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Landlord Proof Obligations

The burden of proof rests with the landlord.

The landlord must demonstrate:

1. the original condition of the property
2. deterioration beyond fair wear and tear
3. financial loss suffered
4. that the proposed deduction is reasonable

Without reliable check-in evidence, claims for cleaning or damage are significantly weakened and may fail entirely. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Cleaning and Wear and Tear

Fair wear and tear applies to condition, not cleanliness.

Tenants are generally expected to return the property cleaned to the same standard as at check-in.

However, landlords cannot claim for a higher cleaning standard than existed at the start of the tenancy. Claims for professional cleaning may fail if the original inventory does not demonstrate professional-level cleanliness. ([mydeposits][2])
