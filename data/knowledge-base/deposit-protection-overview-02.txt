# Tenancy Deposit Protection

## What Should Happen When You Pay Your Deposit?

Most tenancy deposits should be protected within 30 days of payment.

This applies to:

* private assured tenancies from 1 May 2026
* assured shorthold tenancies (ASTs) from 6 April 2007

Most private ASTs became assured tenancies on 1 May 2026.

Lodger deposits do not need to be protected.

Your landlord or letting agent must usually protect your tenancy deposit if you rent privately. This means the deposit must be registered with an approved tenancy deposit protection scheme.

---

## Which Tenancies Does This Apply To?

The deposit protection rules apply to deposits taken for:

* private assured tenancies from 1 May 2026
* assured shorthold tenancies (ASTs) from 6 April 2007

Most private ASTs became assured tenancies on 1 May 2026.

---

## Which Schemes Can Your Landlord Use?

There are 3 approved tenancy deposit protection schemes:

* Deposit Protection Service (DPS)
* Tenancy Deposit Scheme (TDS)
* mydeposits

Your landlord can choose which scheme to use.

Your deposit must remain protected within a scheme for the entire tenancy.

---

## What a Deposit Scheme Does

All 3 schemes provide:

* an insurance option, where the landlord holds the deposit
* a custodial option, where the scheme holds the deposit during the tenancy

The scheme records how much deposit money was paid.

The schemes also provide dispute resolution services. This means you can ask the scheme to decide disagreements about the return of your deposit at the end of the tenancy.

---

## Time Limits to Protect Your Deposit

Your landlord has 30 days from receiving your deposit to:

* register the deposit with a scheme
* give you written information about the protection

Different time limits applied to deposits paid before 6 April 2012.

---

## Written Information From Your Landlord

The written information is known as the prescribed information.

It must include:

* the deposit amount
* the address of the tenancy
* a leaflet from the deposit protection scheme

It must also include the names and contact details of:

* all tenants
* the landlord or letting agent
* the scheme protecting the deposit

The written information should explain:

* how to get the deposit back
* when the landlord can keep deposit money
* how the scheme helps resolve disputes
* what happens if the landlord or agent cannot be contacted or does not respond

The landlord or agent must sign a certificate confirming the information is correct.

---

## Pet Deposits

A landlord may ask for a pet deposit if permission is given to keep a pet.

A pet deposit is still a tenancy deposit. It must usually be protected within a tenancy deposit scheme and should normally be returned at the end of the tenancy.

Damage or cleaning costs caused by pets may be deducted from the tenancy deposit.

Related topics include:

* renting with pets
* when a landlord may keep a deposit

---

## Zero Deposit Schemes

A fee paid for a “zero deposit” option is not treated as a tenancy deposit.

The landlord does not have to protect this money, and tenants do not receive it back at the end of the tenancy.

Some tenants choose zero deposit schemes because the upfront costs are lower. However, tenants cannot be forced to use one of these schemes.
