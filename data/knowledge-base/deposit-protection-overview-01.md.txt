Tenancy Deposit Protection
Overview
Your landlord must put your deposit in a government-approved tenancy deposit scheme (TDP) if you rent your home on an assured shorthold tenancy that started after 6 April 2007.
In England and Wales, your deposit can be registered with one of the following schemes:
* Deposit Protection Service 
* MyDeposits (including deposits previously held by Capita) 
* Tenancy Deposit Scheme 
If you do not rent your home on an assured shorthold tenancy, your landlord can accept valuable items (for example, a car or watch) as a deposit instead of money. These items will not be protected by a tenancy deposit scheme.
Tenancy deposit schemes help make sure you will get your deposit back if you:
* meet the terms of your tenancy agreement 
* do not damage the property 
* pay your rent and bills 
Your landlord or letting agent must place your deposit into a scheme within 30 days of receiving it.

If You’re in Scotland or Northern Ireland
There are separate tenancy deposit protection schemes in Scotland and Northern Ireland.
* TDP schemes in Scotland 
* TDP schemes in Northern Ireland 

At the End of Your Tenancy
Your landlord must return your deposit within 10 days of both parties agreeing how much will be returned.
If there is a dispute between you and your landlord, your deposit remains protected within the tenancy deposit scheme until the dispute is resolved.

Holding Deposits
A landlord does not have to protect a holding deposit.
A holding deposit is money paid to reserve or “hold” a property before a tenancy agreement is signed.
Once you become a tenant, the holding deposit becomes a tenancy deposit and must then be protected within a tenancy deposit scheme.

Deposits Paid by a Third Party
Your landlord must still use a tenancy deposit protection scheme even if the deposit was paid by someone else, such as:
* parents or family members 
* a rent deposit scheme 
* another third party organisation
