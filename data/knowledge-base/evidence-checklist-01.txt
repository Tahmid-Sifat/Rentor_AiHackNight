# Evidence Preparation for Tenancy Deposit Disputes

## Overview

When a tenancy deposit dispute is raised, the tenancy deposit protection scheme will request evidence from both the tenant and the landlord.

The scheme assesses the evidence to decide:

* whether the tenant should receive the full deposit back
* whether the landlord is entitled to keep part of the deposit

Good quality evidence is essential in disputes involving:

* cleaning deductions
* damage claims
* missing items
* unpaid rent or bills
* fair wear and tear

Tenants and landlords should collect and preserve evidence throughout the tenancy wherever possible.

---

# Inventories and Check-Out Reports

## Importance of Inventories

Check-in inventories and check-out inspection reports are among the most important pieces of evidence in tenancy deposit disputes.

These documents help compare:

* the condition of the property at tenancy start
* the condition at tenancy end

Deposit schemes generally consider inventories and check-out reports to be strong evidence.

Where a landlord claims for cleaning or damage, they are expected to show evidence of the property’s condition both before and after the tenancy.

Claims may be rejected where comparative inventory evidence is missing.

---

## Check-Out Inspections

Tenants are encouraged to attend the check-out inspection where possible.

Attending allows tenants to:

* review the inspection findings
* challenge inaccuracies
* ensure comments are properly recorded

Tenants should inform the scheme if:

* they were not invited to the inspection
* the landlord completed the inspection themselves without an independent clerk

The scheme may consider independently prepared reports more reliable than landlord-only inspections.

However, a missing inventory or absent check-out inspection does not automatically prevent a tenant from disputing deductions.

---

# Photographic and Video Evidence

## Videos

Tenants may create a video on the day they move out showing the condition of the property.

Useful video evidence may include:

* room condition
* cleanliness
* visible wear and tear
* pre-existing defects
* unresolved repair issues
* meter readings

Videos should remain factual, clear, and concise.

Tenants may also explain:

* how long they lived in the property
* why deterioration amounts to fair wear and tear
* problems previously reported to the landlord

Move-in videos and photographs may also help establish the original condition of the property.

---

## Photographs

Photographs should clearly explain:

* what room or item is shown
* when the image was taken
* what the image demonstrates

Digital photographs are generally preferred because metadata and dates can often be verified.

Images should clearly show:

* cleanliness
* alleged damage
* deterioration
* defects
* missing items

---

# Communication Records

## Emails and Letters

Written communications between landlords and tenants can be important evidence.

These records may help establish:

* notice periods
* repair reporting
* maintenance delays
* agreements between parties
* landlord responses
* timelines of events

Examples include:

* emails
* letters
* text messages
* messaging app conversations

Communication records may help adjudicators understand responsibility for deterioration or delayed repairs.

---

# Receipts and Supporting Documents

Receipts, invoices, and contractor documents may support claims relating to:

* cleaning
* repairs
* replacements
* gardening
* utility bills

Tenants may also provide receipts showing:

* professional cleaning
* repair work completed during tenancy
* replacement purchases

Supporting documents can help demonstrate whether deductions are reasonable and proportionate.

---

# Witness Statements

Written witness statements may support either party’s position.

Potential witnesses can include:

* contractors
* neighbours
* previous tenants
* inventory clerks
* third-party professionals

Witnesses may confirm:

* pre-existing issues
* property condition
* repair problems
* events during the tenancy

Deposit schemes generally review written witness statements but do not contact witnesses directly for further questioning.

---

# Practical Example: Fair Wear and Tear

A tenant who lived in a property for several years may experience normal deterioration to carpets, flooring, or decoration.

Evidence supporting fair wear and tear may include:

* inventories showing items were already ageing
* photographs showing gradual deterioration
* videos demonstrating cleanliness despite wear
* inspection comments acknowledging ordinary use

Where evidence demonstrates deterioration consistent with ordinary occupation over time, a landlord’s replacement claim may be reduced or rejected.

---

# Key Principle in Deposit Disputes

Deposit disputes are evidence-based.

The landlord must demonstrate:

1. the original condition of the property
2. deterioration beyond fair wear and tear
3. financial loss suffered
4. that deductions are reasonable

Strong comparative evidence — particularly signed inventories, photographs, videos, receipts, and communication records — is central to successful tenancy deposit dispute resolution.
