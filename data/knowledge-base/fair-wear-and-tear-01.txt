# Guide to Deposits, Disputes and Damages

## Overview of Tenancy Deposit Protection

Tenancy deposit protection legislation was introduced in April 2007 for assured shorthold tenancies in England and Wales.

Residential landlords who take a financial deposit must protect it using a government-approved tenancy deposit protection provider.

There are two types of protection:

### Custodial Protection

* The landlord transfers the deposit to the scheme provider.
* The provider holds the deposit for the duration of the tenancy.
* At tenancy end, the deposit is repaid according to agreement between landlord and tenant.
* If there is a dispute, the provider continues holding the disputed amount until adjudication or a court order.

### Insurance-Backed Protection

* The landlord keeps the deposit and pays a fee to insure it through the provider.
* If a dispute arises, the disputed amount should be transferred to the scheme while adjudication takes place.
* The dispute process can still continue even if the landlord initially fails to transfer the disputed amount.

---

# What Happens at the End of a Tenancy

At tenancy end, the landlord and tenant, or a representative such as an independent inventory clerk, should complete a check-out inspection and report.

The check-out report is used to:

* confirm the property condition
* compare against the original inventory
* agree how the deposit should be divided

Most deposits are returned without dispute. Where agreement cannot be reached, the deposit protection provider offers a free dispute resolution service.

---

# What Is Dispute Resolution?

Dispute resolution is a free alternative to court proceedings provided by tenancy deposit schemes.

The process involves:

1. The landlord submitting evidence supporting deductions.
2. The tenant submitting evidence in response.
3. An independent adjudicator reviewing both sides.
4. The adjudicator deciding how the deposit should be distributed.

The process is evidence-based. Parties do not attend hearings and adjudicators do not visit the property.

Some disputes may instead be referred to court, particularly where there are:

* allegations of fraud
* criminal matters
* highly complex disputes

---

# Burden of Proof

The tenancy deposit legally remains the tenant’s money unless the landlord proves entitlement to deductions.

The landlord carries the burden of proof.

Claims are assessed on the civil standard of proof: the balance of probabilities.

The adjudicator must be persuaded that:

* the tenant breached the tenancy agreement
* the landlord suffered financial loss as a result

---

# Evidence Required for Deposit Claims

Good evidence is critical to successful claims and dispute resolution.

## Essential Evidence

### Signed Tenancy Agreement

The tenancy agreement establishes contractual obligations and is essential in most disputes.

Without it, claims are likely to fail.

### Signed Check-In and Check-Out Inventory Reports

Inventories are among the most important documents in deposit disputes.

Strong inventories generally:

* are detailed and descriptive
* include dated photographs
* are signed by tenants
* are prepared independently where possible

Without comparative inventory evidence, claims for cleaning or damage are likely to fail.

### Date-Stamped Photographs or Videos

Photographs should:

* clearly show alleged damage or defects
* include explanatory descriptions
* ideally be date-stamped
* compare condition before and after tenancy

Electronic originals are preferred over photocopies.

### Invoices, Quotes and Receipts

These may support claims relating to:

* cleaning
* repairs
* gardening
* replacement items
* waste removal

Invoices should ideally itemise work completed and associated costs.

Quotes and estimates are weaker evidence than paid invoices but may still be considered.

### Correspondence Between Parties

Useful evidence can include:

* emails
* letters
* text messages
* messaging app conversations

This may help establish timelines, agreements, admissions of liability, or repair reporting.

### Periodic Inspection Reports

Mid-tenancy inspection reports may help demonstrate ongoing condition issues and communications with tenants.

### Witness Statements

Statements from contractors, surveyors, or third parties may support claims, although adjudicators will not interview witnesses directly.

---

# Common Types of Claims

Common deposit claims include:

* rent arrears
* cleaning
* damage to property or items
* gardening
* contractor fees
* outstanding utility bills

For cleaning claims, tenants are only required to return the property to the same standard of cleanliness as at the start of the tenancy.

“Standard cleaning charges” may be rejected if disproportionate.

---

# Fair Wear and Tear

Fair wear and tear refers to deterioration caused by ordinary use and natural ageing.

Adjudicators commonly consider:

* tenancy length
* number and age of occupiers
* quality and age of items
* expected lifespan of fixtures and furnishings

Examples of wear and tear may include:

* minor carpet marks
* gradual fading
* normal scuffs

Negligent damage, such as burns or spills, may justify deductions.

---

# Betterment

Landlords cannot use the deposit to improve the property or obtain “new for old” replacement value.

Adjudicators consider:

* item age
* remaining lifespan
* depreciation
* reasonable contribution towards replacement

For example, if an old carpet is damaged after several years of tenancy, a tenant may only be liable for part of the replacement cost rather than the full amount.

---

# Adjudicator Decisions

Adjudicators are independent and impartial.

Their decisions are:

* based solely on submitted evidence
* final and legally binding unless challenged through court for an error of law or fact

Landlords and tenants may still negotiate and settle disputes privately before a final adjudication decision is issued.
