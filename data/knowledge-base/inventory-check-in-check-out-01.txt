# Tenancy Inventory and Evidence Guidance

## Overview

In tenancy deposit disputes, the quality and reliability of evidence are critical.

Deposit adjudicators assess evidence provided by landlords and tenants to determine whether deductions from a tenancy deposit are justified. Strong documentary and photographic evidence significantly increases the likelihood of a successful claim or defence.

Good record keeping throughout the tenancy can also help prevent disputes before adjudication becomes necessary.

---

# Importance of Inventories

## Check-In and Check-Out Inventory Reports

Signed check-in and check-out inventory reports are among the most important forms of evidence in tenancy deposit disputes.

These reports allow adjudicators to compare:

* the condition of the property at the start of the tenancy
* the condition at the end of the tenancy

Without reliable comparative evidence, claims relating to:

* cleaning
* damage
* missing items
* gardening
* redecoration
* wear and tear

may fail or be significantly weakened.

---

## Signed Reports

Signed inventories carry greater evidential weight because they demonstrate that both parties had the opportunity to review and agree the property condition.

Unsigned reports are harder to verify and may reduce the reliability of the evidence during adjudication.

Reports should ideally:

* be signed by all parties
* clearly describe the condition of each room and item
* include detailed observations
* record cleanliness and existing defects
* contain supporting photographs

---

# Landlord Responsibilities

Landlords and letting agents should:

* prepare accurate check-in inventories at tenancy start
* complete check-out inspections promptly after tenancy end
* retain copies of signed agreements and reports
* maintain written records throughout the tenancy
* gather evidence supporting any proposed deductions

Landlords should also retain:

* invoices
* estimates
* receipts
* inspection reports
* correspondence with tenants

Where deductions are claimed, the landlord carries the burden of proof.

The landlord must demonstrate:

1. the original condition of the property
2. deterioration beyond fair wear and tear
3. financial loss suffered
4. that the deduction claimed is reasonable

---

# Tenant Responsibilities

Tenants should:

* review inventories carefully before signing
* record disagreements or missing information
* retain copies of signed documents
* document property condition during the tenancy and at move-out
* communicate repair or maintenance concerns in writing where possible

Tenants may also submit their own evidence during dispute resolution, including:

* photographs
* emails or text messages
* witness statements
* receipts for cleaning or repairs

---

# Photographic and Video Evidence

## Date-Stamped Images

Date-stamped photographs and videos are highly valuable evidence in deposit disputes.

“Before and after” images allow adjudicators to assess:

* cleaning standards
* damage
* deterioration
* missing items
* property condition over time

Images should:

* clearly show the alleged issue
* be high quality and readable
* include explanations or descriptions where necessary
* identify the relevant room, item, or surface

Electronic originals are generally preferred over photocopies or low-quality reproductions.

---

# Periodic Property Inspection Reports

Mid-tenancy inspection reports can support claims by showing:

* ongoing property condition
* maintenance concerns
* tenant compliance with tenancy obligations
* progression of deterioration or damage

These reports do not need to be as detailed as inventories but may provide useful supporting evidence.

---

# Supporting Documentary Evidence

Additional evidence commonly used in adjudication includes:

* signed tenancy agreements
* invoices and receipts
* repair quotations and estimates
* rent account statements
* contractor reports
* witness statements
* correspondence between parties

Communications such as emails, text messages, and written notices may help adjudicators establish timelines, agreements, repair reporting, or admissions of liability.

---

# Dispute Resolution and Evidence Assessment

Deposit disputes are assessed by independent adjudicators using the evidence submitted by both parties.

Adjudicators do not normally:

* visit the property
* interview witnesses
* gather additional evidence independently

Decisions are based solely on the evidence provided.

The strength, consistency, and completeness of inventories and supporting documentation can therefore significantly influence the outcome of a tenancy deposit dispute.

---

# Key Principle

Reliable signed inventories supported by clear photographic evidence are among the strongest forms of evidence in tenancy deposit adjudication and are often decisive in disputes concerning cleaning, damage, and fair wear and tear.
