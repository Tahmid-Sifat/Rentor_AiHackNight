# What Can a Landlord Keep From Your Deposit?

## Reasons Your Landlord Could Keep Your Deposit

A tenancy deposit belongs to the tenant.

Your landlord or letting agent should return the full deposit at the end of the tenancy unless there is a valid reason to make deductions.

Any deductions must be:

* reasonable
* linked to actual financial loss suffered by the landlord

Common reasons for deductions can include:

* unpaid rent or bills
* cleaning, gardening, or decorating costs
* damage or missing items
* costs caused by leaving early or breaking the tenancy agreement

---

## Return the Property How You Found It

Tenants should return the property in a similar condition to when they moved in.

You are not required to improve the property or return it in better condition than it was at the start of the tenancy.

Some tenants choose to pay for professional cleaning before moving out to help avoid disputes about the deposit, but there is no general legal requirement to pay for professional cleaning.

---

## Fair Wear and Tear

A landlord cannot keep deposit money for fair wear and tear.

Fair wear and tear refers to deterioration caused by normal everyday use of the property over time.

It is not considered to be the tenant’s fault.

Examples of fair wear and tear include:

* small scuffs or marks on walls
* faded curtains or furniture
* worn flooring
* frayed carpets

Landlords and tenants commonly disagree about what qualifies as fair wear and tear.

If there is a dispute, tenancy deposit protection schemes may consider factors such as:

* how long the tenant lived in the property
* the number of people living there
* whether children or pets lived at the property
* the expected lifespan of items and decoration

---

## Example: Normal Wear and Tear in a Family Home

Greater wear and tear may be considered reasonable for a family with children living in a property for five years compared with a single tenant living in the same property for six months.

---

## Deposit Disputes

If a disagreement arises about deductions from a tenancy deposit, tenants and landlords can ask the tenancy deposit protection scheme to resolve the dispute.

The scheme will usually assess evidence such as:

* check-in and check-out inventories
* photographs
* tenancy agreements
* receipts or invoices
* communication between the landlord and tenant

---
