# Evidence Preparation for Tenancy Deposit Disputes

## Overview

Evidence is central to tenancy deposit dispute resolution.

When disagreements arise over proposed deductions from a tenancy deposit, adjudicators rely entirely on the evidence submitted by landlords and tenants. Adjudicators do not normally visit the property or interview the parties involved.

The tenancy deposit legally belongs to the tenant unless the landlord can demonstrate entitlement to deductions through evidence.

Insufficient or incomplete evidence may result in disputed amounts being returned to the tenant.

---

# Role of Comparative Evidence

Comparative evidence is one of the most important principles in deposit adjudication.

Adjudicators compare the condition of the property:

* at the start of the tenancy
* at the end of the tenancy

This allows the adjudicator to assess:

* deterioration
* cleaning standards
* damage
* missing items
* fair wear and tear

Without reliable comparative evidence, claims for cleaning or damage may fail.

---

# Core Evidence Types

## Inventory Reports

An inventory is a detailed written record of the property condition and contents at move-in.

A strong inventory should:

* describe each room, fixture, and fitting
* record existing defects or wear
* record cleanliness standards
* include supporting photographs
* be signed by the tenant and landlord where possible

Where signatures are unavailable, there should be evidence that the tenant received the inventory and had an opportunity to comment on it.

Inventories form the baseline for all later comparison during dispute resolution.

---

## Check-In and Check-Out Reports

Check-in and check-out reports are essential comparative documents.

### Check-In Reports

The check-in report records the property condition at tenancy start and is usually provided when keys are collected.

Tenants are generally given time to review and identify discrepancies.

### Check-Out Reports

The check-out report records the property condition at tenancy end.

The inspection should occur as close as possible to the tenant vacating and returning keys.

Delays in carrying out the inspection may weaken claims because tenants may argue deterioration occurred after they left.

By comparing check-in and check-out evidence, adjudicators assess what changed during the tenancy.

---

# Photographs and Video Evidence

Visual evidence is highly persuasive when clear and properly documented.

## Best Practice for Photographs

Photographs should:

* be date-stamped where possible
* clearly identify the issue
* include both wide-angle context and close-up detail
* be taken in good lighting
* remain clear and in focus

Blurry, dark, or undated photographs may carry less evidential value.

Where photographs are separate from inventory reports, they should be shared with the other party.

---

## Video Evidence

Videos may assist in demonstrating:

* room condition
* cleanliness
* visible defects
* meter readings
* property condition at move-out

Clear narration explaining issues or context may improve evidential clarity.

---

# Tenancy Agreements

A signed tenancy agreement is a core evidential document.

The agreement establishes:

* contractual obligations
* tenant responsibilities
* cleaning obligations
* repair responsibilities
* agreed tenancy terms

Adjudicators may rely on tenancy clauses when assessing whether a claimed deduction is justified.

---

# Invoices, Receipts and Contractor Quotes

Where landlords seek deductions for:

* cleaning
* repairs
* redecoration
* replacement items

they should provide supporting financial evidence.

Relevant documents include:

* invoices
* paid receipts
* contractor estimates
* quotations
* job sheets

Adjudicators assess whether claimed costs are:

* reasonable
* proportionate
* properly itemised

Estimates and completed invoices are both considered during adjudication.

Where deterioration is not rectified immediately, future work estimates may still support a claim.

---

# Communication Records

Written communication can significantly influence dispute outcomes.

Useful records may include:

* emails
* text messages
* WhatsApp conversations
* written permissions
* maintenance discussions
* repair reporting

Communication evidence may help establish:

* timelines
* notice periods
* repair delays
* admissions of liability
* agreements between parties

For example, unresolved repair issues may affect responsibility for resulting damage.

---

# How Adjudicators Assess Evidence

Adjudicators commonly evaluate evidence using four principles:

## Consistency

Do the inventories, photographs, invoices, and communications support the same narrative?

## Credibility

Are the documents reliable, clear, and reasonably independent?

## Completeness

Is key evidence missing that weakens the claim or defence?

## Relevance

Does the evidence directly relate to the disputed issue?

Adjudicators also consider fair wear and tear, ordinary ageing, and proportionality when assessing claims.

---

# Common Evidence Weaknesses

Common issues that weaken disputes include:

* missing inventories
* unsigned reports
* poor-quality photographs
* inconsistent claims
* lack of written communication
* absence of comparative evidence

For example, a cleaning invoice alone may be insufficient if no evidence demonstrates the property was cleaner at check-in.

---

# Evidence Organisation

Evidence should be organised logically and clearly.

Best practice includes:

* arranging communications chronologically
* clearly labelling files
* grouping evidence by issue or claim
* highlighting relevant sections in lengthy documents

Well-organised evidence improves adjudicator review and supports clearer dispute analysis.

---

# Key Principle

Deposit adjudication is evidence-based.

Strong comparative evidence — particularly signed inventories, check-in and check-out reports, photographs, invoices, tenancy agreements, and communication records — is essential for proving or disputing tenancy deposit deductions.
