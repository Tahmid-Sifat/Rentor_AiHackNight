# Tenancy Deposit Dispute Resolution and ADR Procedures

## Overview

Tenancy deposit dispute resolution provides a formal process for resolving disagreements between landlords and tenants about the return of a tenancy deposit at the end of a tenancy.

All authorised tenancy deposit protection schemes in England and Wales provide a free Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) service as an alternative to court proceedings. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

ADR is an evidence-based adjudication process designed to determine how disputed deposit funds should be distributed fairly.

---

# End of Tenancy Process

At the end of the tenancy, the landlord and tenant should inspect the property and compare its condition against the original inventory and tenancy records.

A check-out inspection should ideally be completed as soon as possible after the tenant vacates and returns the keys.

In many cases, the parties agree how the deposit should be repaid and the matter concludes without formal dispute resolution. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

Where agreement cannot be reached, either party may escalate the matter to the tenancy deposit scheme’s ADR process.

---

# Dispute Escalation Process

## Initial Disagreement

Disputes commonly arise concerning:

* cleaning deductions
* damage claims
* missing items
* rent arrears
* gardening or maintenance costs
* fair wear and tear

The parties are encouraged to continue negotiating before adjudication begins.

Many disputes can be resolved through direct communication and clarification of evidence. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

## Referral to ADR

If negotiation fails, the disputed amount of the deposit enters the tenancy deposit scheme’s dispute resolution process.

The scheme will request evidence from both parties within specified deadlines. Each scheme has its own procedural timescales and submission requirements. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

The adjudicator then reviews the submitted evidence and determines how the disputed amount should be allocated.

---

# What ADR Is — and Is Not

ADR in tenancy deposit disputes is:

* independent
* impartial
* evidence-based
* binding once accepted

ADR is not:

* mediation
* counselling
* negotiation between parties

The parties are not normally required to:

* attend hearings
* meet the adjudicator
* provide oral testimony

Adjudicators do not usually visit the property involved in the dispute. Decisions are made solely on documentary and photographic evidence submitted by the parties. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Adjudication Principles

## Balance of Probabilities

Adjudicators decide disputes using the civil standard of proof known as the “balance of probabilities”.

The adjudicator assesses whether the evidence demonstrates that a claimed loss or breach is more likely than not to have occurred. ([watermanresidential.co.uk][2])

---

## Burden of Proof

The tenancy deposit legally remains the tenant’s money unless the landlord proves entitlement to deductions.

The landlord must demonstrate:

1. a breach of the tenancy agreement
2. deterioration beyond fair wear and tear
3. financial loss suffered
4. that the deduction claimed is reasonable

If the landlord cannot provide sufficient evidence, the disputed amount is normally returned to the tenant. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Evidence Review Process

## Evidence Submission

Both landlord and tenant may submit evidence supporting their position.

Common evidence includes:

* signed tenancy agreements
* check-in inventories
* check-out reports
* date-stamped photographs and videos
* invoices and quotations
* rent account statements
* inspection reports
* emails and written communication
* witness statements

Comparative evidence showing the property condition at both the start and end of the tenancy is particularly important. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

## Adjudicator Assessment

Adjudicators evaluate:

* consistency of evidence
* credibility and reliability
* completeness of documentation
* relevance to the disputed issue

The adjudicator may also assess:

* fair wear and tear
* depreciation
* lifespan of items
* proportionality of deductions
* potential betterment

Awards may involve:

* repair costs
* cleaning costs
* partial compensation
* apportioned replacement costs

rather than full replacement value. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])

---

# Landlord Responsibilities

Landlords should:

* protect deposits correctly
* maintain detailed tenancy records
* prepare signed inventories
* document property condition
* retain invoices and receipts
* provide evidence supporting deductions

Landlords should ensure evidence is submitted within scheme deadlines because additional evidence may not normally be accepted later in the process. ([Housing Rights][3])

---

# Tenant Responsibilities

Tenants should:

* review inventories carefully
* retain copies of tenancy documents
* photograph the property before leaving
* attend check-out inspections where possible
* keep written communication records
* challenge deductions promptly if disputed

Tenants may also submit evidence supporting fair wear and tear, pre-existing defects, or unresolved maintenance problems.

---

# Binding Decisions and Court Escalation

ADR decisions are generally final and binding once both parties consent to the process.

Challenges are usually limited to situations involving:

* errors of fact
* errors of law

Certain disputes may be referred to court instead of ADR, particularly where cases involve:

* allegations of fraud
* criminal activity
* highly complex factual disputes

Court proceedings may also remain available where parties decline ADR. ([custodial.tenancydepositscheme.com][1])
