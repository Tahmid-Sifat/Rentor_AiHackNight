# What Makes a Reasonable Deposit Claim?

There are several common reasons a landlord or letting agent may claim money from a tenancy deposit. These generally fall into five categories:

* rent arrears
* cleaning and removal of rubbish or belongings left by the tenant
* repairs and redecoration
* replacement of missing or damaged items
* garden maintenance

Landlords and letting agents must act reasonably and should not claim more than is necessary to compensate for actual loss.

If a dispute arises, adjudicators may consider different remedies, including:

* replacement of items damaged beyond repair
* repair or cleaning where replacement is unnecessary
* compensation where an item’s value or lifespan has been reduced

---

# Betterment

Deposit deductions must not place the landlord in a better position than at the start of the tenancy.

This principle is known as betterment.

Examples of unreasonable betterment can include:

* claiming the full cost of replacing an old carpet after a long tenancy
* replacing a modest item with a premium or prestige brand at the tenant’s expense
* claiming full professional cleaning costs where cleaning defects already existed at check-in

A landlord may only claim a fair contribution towards loss caused by the tenant.

# Fair Wear and Tear

Fair wear and tear refers to deterioration caused by reasonable everyday use of the property and natural ageing.

The House of Lords defines fair wear and tear as:

> “reasonable use of the premises by the tenant and the ordinary operation of natural forces”

There is no strict formula for calculating fair wear and tear. Adjudicators assess disputes based on the circumstances of each tenancy.

Factors commonly considered include:

---

## Length of Tenancy

Longer tenancies naturally result in greater wear and tear.

Adjudicators may consider:

* how long the tenant occupied the property
* how long items had already been in use
* how many previous tenancies the items had gone through

---

## Number and Age of Occupiers

Higher levels of wear are expected in properties occupied by larger households or families with children.

Examples of normal wear can include:

* scuffs and scrapes
* increased appliance usage
* gradual deterioration from family living

A single occupant would generally be expected to cause less wear.

---

## Lifespan, Quality and Condition of Items

The age, quality and durability of items are relevant when assessing deductions.

Lower-cost or older items may deteriorate more quickly through ordinary use.

Adjudicators may request evidence such as:

* purchase receipts
* invoices
* proof of original quality and age

The quality of the property itself may also be relevant. Less durable finishes or materials may require more frequent redecoration.

---

## Wear and Tear vs Actual Damage

Fair wear and tear differs from negligent or accidental damage.

Examples likely to be considered normal wear and tear:

* light carpet marks
* gradual fading
* minor surface scuffs

Examples more likely to be considered tenant damage:

* nail varnish spills
* iron burns
* negligent breakages
* missing items
* burnt-out fittings caused by misuse

Adjudicators assess whether deterioration occurred through ordinary use or negligence.

---

# Keeping Good Records

Good quality evidence is essential when making or disputing a deposit claim.

Written records and supporting documents may help resolve disputes or avoid them entirely.

Important evidence can include:

* signed tenancy agreements
* signed check-in and check-out inventory reports
* periodic inspection reports
* correspondence between landlord and tenant
* invoices, receipts, estimates and quotations
* rent account statements for arrears claims
* date-stamped photographs or videos
* witness statements from contractors or third parties

Signed inventory reports generally carry greater evidential weight than unsigned reports.

Photographs and videos should clearly show the alleged issue and include explanations where necessary.

Invoices and receipts should ideally itemise work completed and associated costs.

---

# Evidence Commonly Used in Adjudication

Adjudicators commonly review:

* tenancy agreements
* inventories
* inspection reports
* photographs
* repair invoices
* rent statements
* communications between parties
* contractor or witness statements

The quality and completeness of evidence can significantly affect the outcome of a tenancy deposit dispute.
