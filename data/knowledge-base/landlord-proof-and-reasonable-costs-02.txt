# Tenancy Deposit Deductions: Reasonable Claims and Landlord Proof Obligations

## Overview

A tenancy deposit remains the tenant’s money unless the landlord or letting agent can demonstrate a valid reason for making deductions.

Any deduction must:

* be reasonable
* relate to actual financial loss
* be supported by evidence

Landlords cannot automatically retain part of a deposit simply because cleaning, repairs, or redecoration are required after the tenancy ends.

---

# What Landlords Must Prove

In tenancy deposit disputes, the landlord generally carries the burden of proof.

To justify deductions, the landlord should demonstrate:

1. the original condition of the property at the start of the tenancy
2. deterioration or loss beyond fair wear and tear
3. that the tenant caused or was responsible for the issue
4. the financial loss suffered
5. that the amount claimed is reasonable and proportionate

Without sufficient evidence, disputed deductions may be rejected by the tenancy deposit scheme.

---

# Evidence Standards

Deposit disputes are evidence-based.

Strong evidence commonly includes:

* signed tenancy agreements
* check-in inventories
* check-out inspection reports
* dated photographs or videos
* invoices and receipts
* communication records
* contractor quotations

Comparative evidence showing the property condition before and after the tenancy is particularly important.

Where landlords cannot demonstrate the original condition of the property, claims for cleaning or damage may fail.

---

# Returning the Property

Tenants are generally expected to return the property in a similar condition to when they moved in.

However, tenants are not required to:

* improve the property
* return ageing items to “as new” condition
* pay for ordinary deterioration caused by normal living

Professional cleaning is not automatically required unless the tenancy agreement and property condition justify it.

---

# Fair Wear and Tear

## Definition

Fair wear and tear refers to deterioration caused by ordinary everyday use over time.

It is not treated as tenant damage.

Examples can include:

* small scuffs on walls
* faded curtains or furnishings
* worn flooring
* frayed carpets

Landlords cannot normally deduct money from the deposit for fair wear and tear.

---

# Factors Considered in Wear and Tear Disputes

Adjudicators commonly consider:

* length of tenancy
* number of occupants
* whether children lived at the property
* expected lifespan of fixtures and furnishings
* ordinary use of the property

For example, greater wear may reasonably be expected after a five-year family tenancy than after a short single-occupancy tenancy.

---

# Betterment Principle

Landlords must not use tenancy deposit deductions to improve the property at the tenant’s expense.

This principle is known as betterment.

A tenant should not be charged the full replacement cost of an old or partially depreciated item where ordinary ageing has already reduced its value.

Examples of potential betterment include:

* replacing an old carpet with a brand-new one at full tenant cost
* redecorating an entire room due to minor marks
* upgrading fixtures beyond their original standard

Adjudicators may reduce claims where deductions would leave the landlord in a better position than at tenancy start.

---

# Practical Deduction Examples

## Potentially Reasonable Deductions

The following may justify deductions where supported by evidence:

* unpaid rent or bills
* significant carpet stains
* broken furniture or missing items
* negligent damage
* excessive dirt or rubbish removal
* garden neglect beyond ordinary seasonal growth

---

## Potentially Unreasonable Deductions

The following may be rejected or reduced:

* replacing worn carpets affected by ordinary ageing
* charging for minor scuffs after long occupation
* full-room redecoration for limited marks
* charging for deterioration caused by unresolved landlord repairs
* requiring “better than move-in” condition

---

# Cleaning Claims

Cleaning claims must usually show that:

* the property was cleaner at check-in
* the tenant failed to return it to a similar standard
* the cleaning costs claimed are proportionate

Landlords cannot normally charge for professional cleaning where:

* comparable cleaning standards did not exist at tenancy start
* deterioration results from ordinary use rather than neglect

---

# Damage Claims

Landlords must distinguish between:

* ordinary wear and tear
* actual tenant-caused damage

Examples of tenant damage may include:

* spills causing permanent staining
* broken fixtures
* negligent burns or marks
* missing inventory items

Claims should reflect the actual financial loss suffered rather than automatic replacement costs.

---

# Key Principle in Deposit Adjudication

Tenancy deposit adjudicators assess whether deductions are reasonable, evidence-based, and proportionate.

Successful claims generally require clear comparative evidence showing deterioration beyond fair wear and tear together with proof of actual financial loss.
