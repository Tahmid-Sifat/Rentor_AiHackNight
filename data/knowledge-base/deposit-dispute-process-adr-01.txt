# Tenancy Deposit Dispute Resolution and ADR Guide

## Overview

Tenants who paid a tenancy deposit generally have the right to receive it back at the end of the tenancy unless the landlord or letting agent has a valid reason to make deductions.

Common reasons for deductions may include:

* unpaid rent
* damage to the property
* missing items from the inventory

Landlords cannot normally deduct money for reasonable wear and tear, such as gradual deterioration through ordinary use.

Where disagreements arise about deductions, tenancy deposit protection schemes provide dispute resolution processes, including Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR).

---

# Requesting the Deposit Return

At the end of the tenancy, tenants should contact the landlord or letting agent and request the return of the deposit.

Written communication is recommended because it creates evidence of:

* when the request was made
* what was discussed
* any proposed deductions

If both parties agree how much should be returned, repayment is usually made within a couple of weeks.

---

# Preparing Evidence Before Moving Out

Evidence is important in tenancy deposit disputes.

Tenants are encouraged to gather evidence showing the property condition at move-out.

Useful evidence may include:

* photographs of each room
* videos showing cleanliness and condition
* check-out inventories
* signed inspection reports
* communication records
* receipts or invoices for cleaning or repairs

Tenants should ideally ask the landlord or agent to sign the check-out inventory.

---

# Valid and Invalid Deposit Deductions

## Deductions a Landlord May Claim

Landlords or agents may usually deduct money where:

* rent is unpaid
* the tenant caused damage
* items are lost or broken

Examples may include:

* carpet stains
* damaged walls
* broken inventory items

Landlords should explain deductions clearly and preferably in writing.

---

## Deductions a Landlord Cannot Usually Claim

Landlords cannot normally charge tenants for:

* reasonable wear and tear
* gradual ageing of fixtures or decoration
* betterment or upgrades

Examples include:

* replacing an old carpet due to ordinary ageing
* repainting an entire room because of minor scuff marks
* damage caused by repairs the landlord failed to complete

---

# Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)

## What Is ADR?

Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) is the free dispute resolution service provided by tenancy deposit protection schemes.

ADR is used when:

* the landlord and tenant disagree about deductions
* the deposit is protected within a tenancy deposit scheme

ADR is intended as an alternative to court proceedings.

---

## How ADR Works

The dispute process generally involves:

1. The tenant disputes proposed deductions.
2. Both parties submit evidence to the tenancy deposit scheme.
3. An independent adjudicator reviews the evidence.
4. The adjudicator decides how the deposit should be distributed.

The adjudicator does not normally:

* visit the property
* interview parties in person
* collect evidence independently

The decision is based entirely on submitted evidence.

---

# Evidence in ADR

The tenancy deposit scheme reviews evidence from both landlord and tenant.

Important evidence may include:

* tenancy agreements
* inventories
* check-in and check-out reports
* photographs and videos
* receipts and invoices
* emails and written communication

Comparative evidence showing the property condition at both the start and end of the tenancy is especially important.

---

# Burden of Proof

The landlord generally carries the burden of proof.

The landlord must demonstrate:

1. the original condition of the property
2. deterioration beyond fair wear and tear
3. financial loss suffered
4. that deductions are reasonable

Without sufficient evidence, disputed deposit amounts may be returned to the tenant.

---

# Escalation Process

## Initial Negotiation

Tenants should first try to resolve disagreements directly with the landlord or agent.

Tenants may request:

* written explanations
* quotations or invoices
* evidence supporting deductions

---

## Formal Deposit Dispute

If agreement cannot be reached and the deposit is protected, the tenant may escalate the matter through the tenancy deposit scheme’s dispute resolution service.

Main tenancy deposit schemes include:

* Deposit Protection Service (DPS)
* Tenancy Deposit Scheme (TDS)
* mydeposits

The scheme will request evidence from both parties during the adjudication process.

---

# Deposits Protected vs Unprotected

The dispute process depends on whether the deposit was protected within a tenancy deposit scheme.

Landlords are generally required to protect deposits for assured tenancies and assured shorthold tenancies.

If the deposit is not protected, different legal remedies may apply.

---

# Practical Guidance for Tenants

Tenants should:

* keep written records throughout the tenancy
* photograph the property before leaving
* attend check-out inspections where possible
* request written reasons for deductions
* retain copies of agreements and inventories
* dispute deductions promptly if they appear unreasonable

Strong documentary and photographic evidence can significantly improve a tenant’s position during ADR and deposit dispute resolution.
