# Inventories and Check-In / Check-Out Procedures in Tenancy Deposit Disputes

Inventories and check-in/check-out reports are some of the most important forms of evidence in tenancy deposit disputes. Deposit protection schemes rely heavily on these documents when deciding whether a landlord is entitled to keep part of a tenant’s deposit for cleaning, damage, or missing items.

A landlord who wishes to make deductions from a tenancy deposit must normally show evidence of the property’s condition at both the start and end of the tenancy. Signed inventories, inspection reports, photographs, videos, and written records are commonly used for this purpose.

## Role of Inventories in Deposit Disputes

An inventory is a written record describing the condition and contents of a property when the tenant moves in. It may include descriptions of rooms, fixtures, furniture, decoration, appliances, and levels of cleanliness.

Inventories are important because they provide a baseline against which the property’s condition at the end of the tenancy can be compared.

Deposit protection schemes consider inventories and checkout reports to be strong evidence in disputes involving:

- cleaning deductions
- property damage
- missing items
- decoration issues
- furniture condition

Where no inventory exists, it may become difficult for a landlord to prove that deterioration occurred during the tenancy.

## Check-In Reports

A check-in report is normally completed when the tenant moves into the property. It records the property’s condition at the beginning of the tenancy and is often linked to the inventory.

For evidence purposes, check-in reports are strongest when:

- signed by both landlord and tenant
- completed before or on move-in day
- supported by photographs or videos
- detailed and room-specific

Tenants should review the report carefully and raise any disagreements or missing issues immediately.

## Check-Out Reports

A checkout report records the condition of the property after the tenant leaves. Deposit schemes compare the checkout report with the original inventory and check-in evidence to identify any changes.

Tenants are encouraged to attend checkout inspections where possible so they can:

- confirm observations
- challenge inaccurate notes
- provide immediate explanations
- ensure comments are recorded fairly

A checkout report completed solely by the landlord or agent may sometimes be considered less reliable than one prepared independently.

If no checkout inspection takes place, the landlord may face difficulties proving deterioration or cleaning issues.

## Photographic and Video Evidence

Photographs and videos can provide strong supporting evidence when clearly dated and properly explained.

Useful evidence practices include:

- taking photographs on move-in and move-out days
- recording every room and major fixture
- using clear lighting and high-quality images
- explaining what each image shows
- retaining original digital timestamps where possible

Videos may also help demonstrate:

- normal wear and tear
- existing defects
- unresolved repairs
- overall cleanliness at the end of the tenancy

Meter readings and appliance conditions may also be recorded during move-out.

## Landlord and Tenant Responsibilities

Landlords are generally responsible for maintaining accurate inventories and proving claims for deductions. If deductions are proposed, the landlord should provide evidence showing:

- the original condition of the property
- deterioration during the tenancy
- reasonable repair or cleaning costs

Tenants are responsible for returning the property in a reasonably clean condition and avoiding avoidable damage.

Tenants should also keep copies of:

- tenancy agreements
- inventories
- repair communications
- photographs
- emails and letters

These documents may help challenge unfair deductions.

## Practical Dispute Considerations

Deposit protection schemes assess evidence from both parties when disputes arise. Adjudicators do not usually inspect properties in person and instead rely entirely on submitted documentation.

Good evidence is typically:

- clear
- dated
- consistent
- relevant to the dispute
- supported by comparative records

Claims may be weakened by:

- missing inventories
- poor quality photographs
- inconsistent reports
- lack of written communication
- absence of comparative evidence

## Example Scenario

A tenant lives in a property for several years and the living room carpet becomes worn in high-traffic areas. The landlord later proposes a deduction for carpet replacement.

If the inventory confirms the carpet was new at move-in, and the tenant provides photographs and videos showing only gradual deterioration, the evidence may support a finding of fair wear and tear rather than tenant-caused damage.

In tenancy deposit disputes, organised and comparative evidence remains one of the most important factors in achieving a fair outcome.
